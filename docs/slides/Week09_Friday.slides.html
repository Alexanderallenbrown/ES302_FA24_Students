A07 tips¶

Three ways to measure position and orientation (yaw) of Romi:

  1. Ground truth from supervisor
  2. Using measurements from the encoders to get $\omega_l,\omega_r$, then using Odometry
  3. Using A06 model to map input voltages (in counts -> volts) to get predictions of $\omega_r,\omega_l$, and then using those predicted angular velocities to perform Odometry

A07 asks you to compare all three of these methods! Remember that your model predicts forward speed $U$ and angular velocity $\dot{\psi}$ directly. But then, we know that:

$$\dot{\psi} = \frac{\omega_rR_{wheel} - \omega_lR_{wheel}}{2R_{romi}}$$

$$U = R_{wheel}\frac{\omega_l+\omega_r}{2}$$

This means that given a predicted $U$ and a predicted $\dot{\psi}$ at any moment in time, you can compute $\omega_l$ and $\omega_r$. The assignment suggests recording input voltages and time in one file, and raw encoder values in another. This means you'd do all predictions and odometry calcs "offline" after the simulation is over... but this is your choice.

Preparing for the workshop¶

Review your peers' work before class on Wednesday. No Exceptions. Come prepared. Create a document of your own (not shared) that acts as a review.

  1. Focus on concerns "of the highest order." This means focus on content (claims and support and synthesis) over grammar and spelling.
  2. Your private comments, which you will distill and offer to your colleagues, should be supported with specific examples from the text of the lit review. Avoid making general statements.
  3. Offer critique over summary. The expectation is that everyone in your pod will know your papers. Focus on what is done well, and what can be improved.
  4. Be kind, but offer critique. Being mean is unhelpful, and offering blanket support of your peers' work is also unhelpful.

On the day of the workshop¶

You will spend approximately 25 minutes discussing each paper (total 75 minutes). For each paper:

  1. Discuss the strengths of the lit review in question
  2. Share what you think of the RQs of the lit review and how well they are answered. How well do the papers in the lit review support answering the RQ? How well does the lit review synthesize the work to help answer the RQ?
  3. What can be improved about the lit review? Work your way from "high order" concerns to "low order" concerns. Fully discuss each issue raised before moving to the next one, using specific examples from the lit review.
  4. Once all issues have been raised, the author should ask any appropriate follow-up questions to clarify what should be done to improve the lit review.

Make notes in the document itself so that I can track what issues were raised. Do Not "resolve" these comments. Make a new copy of your paper in your A05 folder when working to resolve comments.

Class exercise 1: Make Romi move with a specified forward velocity and yaw rate.¶

If we wanted Romi to move in a circle of radius $R$ at some foward speed $U$, we could compute a goal yaw rate for the robot as:

\begin{equation} \psi_g = \frac{U}{R} \end{equation}

This means that we could specify a "goal" for the Romi's body-fixed task space as:

\begin{equation} \vec{q}_g = \begin{bmatrix}^B\dot{x}_g & ^B\dot{y}_g & ^B\dot{\psi}_g\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}U & 0 & \frac{U}{R}\end{bmatrix} \end{equation}

Then, we could make the Romi achieve this goal in its "body-fixed task space" by moving its wheels at:

\begin{equation} \vec{q} = \begin{bmatrix}\omega_R \\ \omega_L\end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} \frac{R_w}{2} & \frac{R_w}{2} \\ 0 & 0 \\\frac{R_w}{2r} & -\frac{R_w}{2r} \end{bmatrix}^+}_{J^+}\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}U \\ 0 \\ \frac{U}{R}\end{bmatrix}}_{\vec{\mathcal{V}}_b} \end{equation}

You can use the constant 400/15.7 to approximately map motor voltage in "counts" to steady state wheel speed in rad/s.

Class exercise 1: Make Romi move with a specified forward velocity and yaw rate.¶

In the Week09_World in Webots, implement inverse differential kinematics as shown above to achieve any given yaw rate and any given forward speed. There is a "Starter Controller" in that world for your use. You will need to:

  • Calculate wheel speeds
  • compute yaw rate
  • compute forward Romi speed
  • Compute the Jacobian
  • Compute the pseudoinverse of the Jacobian
  • Compute wheel speeds needed to achieve the desired yaw rate and forward velocity, ultimately achieving your goal turn radius $R$.

Finally, use the supervisor and/or your measurements of true robot motion (from odometry and wheel encoder speed measurements) to compare Romi's actual motion with your goal for its motion.

Class exercise 2: Make the Romi reach a desired location using feedback control¶

  • Specify a goal location in the Romi's world
  • Use feedback control to get commanded forward velocity and yaw rate for the Romi (use odometry estimates!)
  • Use inverse differential kinematics to turn those commands into Romi wheel speeds

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} U &= K_U \sqrt{(^Ax_g-^Ax)^2+(^Ay_g-^Ay)^2} \\ \dot{\psi} &= K_\psi \left(\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{^Ay_g-^Ay}{^Ax_g-^Ax}\right) - \psi\right) \end{aligned} \end{equation}
